# Stargate's $500 Billion Bet: Inside the Largest AI Infrastructure Project in History

**Beat:** Infrastructure
**Format:** Deep Dive
**Published:** March 9, 2026
**Read time:** 12 min

**Excerpt:** The Stargate joint venture — OpenAI, SoftBank, Oracle, and MGX — has committed over $400 billion across six US sites with nearly 7 GW of planned capacity. But months of partner disputes over control and ownership nearly derailed the project before it began. Here's where it stands.

---

On January 21, 2025, President Donald Trump stood alongside Sam Altman, Masayoshi Son, and Larry Ellison to announce the Stargate Project — a new company that would invest $500 billion over four years to build AI infrastructure for OpenAI in the United States [1]. It was, by any measure, the most ambitious infrastructure commitment in corporate history.

Fourteen months later, the picture is more complicated than the announcement suggested. The flagship Abilene, Texas campus is partially operational, with NVIDIA GB200 racks running early training and inference workloads [2]. Five additional US sites have been announced [3]. A $30 billion UAE campus is under construction in Abu Dhabi [4]. And Oracle has signed a $300 billion, five-year compute deal that anchors the project's economics [5].

But between the announcement and the buildout, Stargate nearly stalled. Disputes between OpenAI, SoftBank, and Oracle over who would own the data centers, who would control their design, and who would fund construction caused months of delays [6]. The three-way partnership that looked so commanding on the White House lawn devolved into a tug of war that revealed the fundamental tensions in building AI infrastructure at nation-state scale.

This is the story of where Stargate stands — what's built, what's planned, and what nearly went wrong.

## The Abilene Flagship: 1.2 GW and 450,000 GPUs

The first Stargate campus in Abilene, Texas — designated Stargate I — is the project's anchor site and the largest single AI data center currently under construction in the world.

When complete, the facility will consist of 10 buildings spanning approximately 10 million square feet, drawing 1.2 GW of electrical power — enough to serve roughly one million American homes [7]. The campus will house over 450,000 NVIDIA GB200 GPUs, with each building containing up to 50,000 GB200 NVL72 racks [7].

The first two buildings are already operational. Each spans 980,000 square feet with 200 MW of capacity [7]. Oracle began delivering NVIDIA GB200 racks in June 2025, and operations began running early training and inference workloads by September [2]. The remaining eight buildings are expected to be completed by mid-2026 [7].

The facility is being developed by Crusoe Energy, which brings its own power generation expertise to the project [7]. Crusoe's involvement reflects a broader trend: AI data center operators are increasingly partnering with or acquiring energy companies rather than relying on grid connections alone.

## The Five New Sites

In September 2025, OpenAI announced five additional US data center sites under the Stargate umbrella [3], bringing the project's total planned capacity to nearly 7 gigawatts and over $400 billion in investment over three years [3]:

**Shackelford County, Texas:** A second major Texas site, approximately 150 miles from Abilene, leveraging the state's favorable permitting environment and abundant land.

**Doña Ana County, New Mexico:** Located near Las Cruces, this site benefits from proximity to both Texas grid interconnections and New Mexico's renewable energy resources.

**Lordstown, Ohio:** Built on the site of a former General Motors assembly plant, this project repurposes industrial land for AI infrastructure — a pattern that is becoming common as legacy manufacturing sites offer large footprints with existing grid connections.

**Milam County, Texas:** A third Texas location, reflecting the state's emergence as the epicenter of US AI data center construction due to relatively few permitting barriers and available power.

**Undisclosed Midwest site:** A sixth location that has not been publicly identified, reportedly in advanced planning.

The geographic distribution is telling. Three of the six sites are in Texas, which has become the default jurisdiction for large-scale AI data center construction. Texas offers a deregulated energy market (ERCOT), no state income tax, abundant land, and a permitting environment that can approve projects in months rather than years.

## The Partner Disputes

The public narrative of Stargate as a unified national project concealed months of internal conflict among the partners [6].

**The ownership question:** OpenAI initially wanted to own the data centers outright [6]. Owning infrastructure would reduce long-term costs and eliminate dependence on third-party cloud providers — a strategic priority for a company that has historically rented compute from Microsoft Azure. But OpenAI couldn't get financing. Lenders were unwilling to back billion-dollar data center projects from a company with heavy losses and an unproven business model [6].

**The SoftBank impasse:** OpenAI and SoftBank reached a standoff over ownership percentages, funding commitments, and construction control [8]. SoftBank, which committed to providing a significant share of the capital, wanted ownership stakes commensurate with its financial contribution. OpenAI wanted operational control over facility design and compute allocation.

The compromise: OpenAI will sign long-term leases and control each facility's design and compute configuration, while SoftBank's subsidiary SoftBank Energy will develop and own the physical infrastructure [8]. This structure mirrors how many hyperscalers operate — leasing data center shells from developers while controlling the IT buildout.

**The Oracle deal:** Oracle emerged as the infrastructure partner by offering to build and operate the data centers using its Oracle Cloud Infrastructure (OCI) platform [5]. The $300 billion, five-year compute purchase agreement between OpenAI and Oracle anchors the project's revenue model and gave Oracle the scale to justify its own $50 billion capex plan for 2026 [9].

The net result of the disputes was that OpenAI fell short of its goal of locking down 10 GW of capacity commitments by the end of 2025 [6]. The project is now back on track, but the delays cost several months of construction time across the newer sites.

## The Oracle-Stargate Economics

Oracle's role in Stargate deserves particular attention because it represents a company-defining transformation.

In July 2025, OpenAI and Oracle entered an agreement to develop up to 4.5 GW of additional Stargate capacity — a commitment exceeding $300 billion over five years [5]. Combined with the Abilene campus, Oracle's total Stargate exposure approaches the largest single-customer infrastructure deal in history.

Oracle's remaining performance obligations surged to $523 billion — a 438% year-over-year increase driven largely by Stargate commitments [9]. The company's projected $50 billion in 2026 capex represents a 136% increase over 2025, funded by this backlog [9].

The risk is proportional to the scale. If Oracle successfully brings the first 1.5 GW of Stargate capacity online by late 2026, it will have transformed from an enterprise software company into a top-five AI infrastructure provider. If Stargate's buildout encounters further delays or if OpenAI's compute demand falls short of projections, Oracle faces a capital overhang that could strain its balance sheet for years.

## Stargate UAE: The International Expansion

The Stargate concept has expanded beyond US borders. In partnership with G42, Oracle, NVIDIA, Cisco, and SoftBank — and developed in coordination with the US government — Stargate UAE will build a 1 GW compute cluster in Abu Dhabi [4].

The campus will span 19.2 square kilometers — more than nine times the size of Monaco [10]. Total construction cost is estimated at more than $30 billion [10]. The first 200 MW phase is expected to go live in Q3 2026 [11].

Stargate UAE is part of OpenAI's broader "OpenAI for Countries" initiative — a program to help governments build sovereign AI capability using US technology, in coordination with Washington [4]. The UAE project is funded in part by MGX, an Abu Dhabi sovereign wealth fund that is also an equity partner in Stargate LLC [1].

The geopolitical dimension is significant. By channeling AI infrastructure investment through US-aligned partnerships, the Stargate model attempts to create an alternative to China's BRI-style technology infrastructure diplomacy. The US government's involvement in approving and coordinating the UAE deal reflects a recognition that AI compute infrastructure is now a tool of geopolitical influence.

## The Power Problem

Stargate's nearly 7 GW of planned US capacity, plus 1 GW in the UAE, represents an enormous energy commitment. To put 7 GW in context: it exceeds the total generating capacity of many US states and roughly equals the output of seven large nuclear power plants running at full capacity.

Securing this power is the project's single largest execution risk. Each site requires not just electrical generation but grid interconnection, transmission capacity, and backup power — all of which face multi-year lead times. The choice of Texas for three sites reflects ERCOT's relatively faster interconnection process, but even ERCOT is straining under the weight of data center demand.

Crusoe Energy's involvement at the Abilene site points to one solution: bringing dedicated power generation to the data center rather than relying on the grid. Crusoe has experience deploying modular power systems, including natural gas generators, at remote locations. This approach trades energy efficiency for speed — a worthwhile tradeoff when the constraint is time, not cost.

## What Stargate Means for the Supply Chain

Stargate's scale creates massive downstream demand:

**NVIDIA:** The Abilene campus alone requires 450,000 GB200 GPUs [7]. As sites transition to Vera Rubin, the total GPU demand across all Stargate sites could reach into the millions of units — a significant fraction of NVIDIA's total production capacity.

**TSMC:** Every NVIDIA GPU is manufactured by TSMC. Stargate's demand for advanced logic chips (N3P for Rubin) and CoWoS-L packaging directly impacts TSMC's capacity allocation for other customers.

**Memory:** At 288 GB of HBM4 per Rubin GPU, Stargate's memory consumption will represent a meaningful share of global HBM4 production — creating additional pressure on SK Hynix, Samsung, and Micron.

**Cooling:** At 1.2 GW per campus, liquid cooling is mandatory. Stargate sites will be among the largest deployments of direct liquid cooling in the world, driving demand for Vertiv, CoolIT, and other thermal management providers.

**Construction:** The sheer physical scale — tens of millions of square feet across six US sites plus Abu Dhabi — requires construction capacity that competes with other large infrastructure projects. Labor, materials, and heavy equipment face their own supply constraints.

## The $500 Billion Question

Stargate's stated goal of $500 billion in investment over four years would make it the largest privately funded infrastructure project in history — exceeding the Interstate Highway System in inflation-adjusted terms. Whether that number is reached depends on three factors:

**Demand:** OpenAI's compute needs are growing rapidly, but $500 billion implies a scale of AI deployment that exceeds anything currently projected. If OpenAI's revenue continues its trajectory (approximately $20 billion ARR at the end of 2025 [12], with some projections of $100 billion+ by 2028), the spending could be justified. If AI adoption slows, the project could be right-sized.

**Power:** Securing 7+ GW of reliable power across multiple sites is a multi-year challenge that no private entity has attempted at this scale. Grid interconnection delays, permitting disputes, and generation construction timelines all create execution risk.

**Partnership stability:** The partner disputes of 2025 demonstrated that a three-way joint venture building infrastructure at this scale generates friction. The ownership and control compromises may hold, or they may require renegotiation as conditions change.

What is not in question is Stargate's ambition. If even half of the planned capacity is built, it will represent the largest concentration of AI compute infrastructure on the planet — and a bet that artificial intelligence will become an industry requiring physical infrastructure on the scale of energy, transportation, and telecommunications.
