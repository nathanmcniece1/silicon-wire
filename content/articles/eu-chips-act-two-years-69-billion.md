# The EU Chips Act at Two Years: €69 Billion Mobilized, Strategic Gaps Remain

**Beat:** Policy & Capital
**Format:** Deep Dive
**Published:** March 9, 2026
**Read time:** 13 min

**Excerpt:** Two years after entering force, the EU Chips Act has catalyzed €69 billion in public and private semiconductor investment. But the European Court of Auditors projects the EU will reach only 11.7% global market share by 2030 — far short of the 20% target. Intel Magdeburg is cancelled. The STMicroelectronics/GlobalFoundries French fab is shelved. And Chips Act 2.0 is already in the works.

---

On September 21, 2023, the European Chips Act entered into force with an ambitious mandate: double the EU's share of global semiconductor production from approximately 10% to 20% by 2030 [1]. Two years later, the legislation has catalyzed €69 billion in public and private investment, making the EU the third-largest destination for semiconductor capital globally [2]. But the European Court of Auditors has delivered a sobering assessment: the 20% target is "very unlikely to be sufficient," and the EU's actual market share is projected to reach only 11.7% by 2030 [3].

The Chips Act is not a failure. It is an insufficient success — a legislation that mobilized real money and created real capabilities but whose ambitions were scaled to match a geopolitical moment rather than an industrial reality.

## The Three-Pillar Architecture

The Chips Act is structured around three pillars [1].

**Pillar I — Chips for Europe Initiative** funds large-scale technological capacity building in research, design, and manufacturing through 2030. The European Commission allocated approximately €4 billion in direct funding, drawing from existing programs including Horizon Europe and the Digital Europe Programme. Pillar I supports five pilot lines that bridge the gap between laboratory research and industrial production.

**Pillar II — Investment Framework** creates incentives for public and private investment in semiconductor manufacturing facilities, particularly "First of a Kind" (FOAK) facilities that bring new manufacturing capability to Europe. Pillar II operates primarily through member state aid, with the Commission approving state subsidies for qualifying projects.

**Pillar III — Coordination Mechanism** established the European Semiconductor Board to coordinate between the Commission, member states, and industry stakeholders on supply chain monitoring, crisis response, and strategic planning.

A critical distinction from the US CHIPS Act: the EU's funding is largely drawn from existing EU budget lines and member state contributions, not new appropriations. Only approximately €3.3 billion represents genuinely new EU budget money [1]. The remainder comes from member state treasuries, private investment, and the reallocation of existing EU programs. This fragmented funding structure — where the Commission controls roughly €4.5 billion while member states manage the bulk through state aid — is both a political necessity and a structural weakness.

## Where the €69 Billion Went

As of October 2025, the Chips Act had catalyzed €69 billion in cumulative investment across three streams [2]:

**IPCEI ME-CT (Important Projects of Common European Interest — Microelectronics and Communication Technologies):** €21.8 billion total, comprising €8.1 billion from 14 member states and €13.7 billion in private investment [2]. The program funds 68 research and development projects from 56 companies, spanning 5G/6G communications, autonomous driving, AI, and quantum computing. Participating member states include Austria, Czechia, Finland, France, Germany, Greece, Ireland, Italy, Malta, Netherlands, Poland, Romania, Slovakia, and Spain.

**R&D Pilot Lines:** €5.1 billion across five pilot lines [2]. The flagship is NanoIC at IMEC in Leuven, Belgium — Europe's largest Chips Act pilot line, which launched in February 2026 with €700 million in EU funding as part of a total €2.5 billion facility investment [4]. NanoIC is the first European facility deploying state-of-the-art EUV lithography for manufacturing chips beyond 2nm. The four additional pilot lines — FAMES, APECS, WBG, and PIXEurope — cover advanced packaging, power electronics, and photonic integrated circuits.

**First-of-a-Kind Manufacturing Facilities:** €31.4 billion across seven validated FOAK projects [2], with approximately 40% funded through state aid. This category includes the largest individual investments, including TSMC's Dresden fab.

## The Court of Auditors' Verdict

European Court of Auditors Special Report 12/2025 provides the most authoritative assessment of the Chips Act's trajectory [3]. The title summarizes the finding: "Reasonable progress in its implementation but the Chips Act is very unlikely to be sufficient to reach the overly ambitious Digital Decade target."

The auditors' own forecasting projects Europe's global semiconductor value chain share will increase from 9.8% in 2022 to 11.7% by 2030 [3] — a meaningful improvement but far short of the 20% target. The gap reflects several structural challenges:

The Chips Act was drafted without a full impact assessment before adoption [3]. There was no demand analysis to determine whether €69 billion — or any realistic sum — could achieve a 20% global share given the simultaneous massive investments by the US, Japan, South Korea, and China.

The Commission controls only approximately €4.5 billion directly, while member states manage the bulk of funding through state aid mechanisms that the Commission has limited ability to coordinate [3]. The result is a patchwork of national programs with varying timelines, eligibility criteria, and strategic priorities.

Pillar I (R&D) is progressing but experiencing delays. Pillar II (FOAK investments) has had slow uptake, with fewer qualifying projects than anticipated. Pillar III (coordination and crisis monitoring) remains in early stages [3].

The auditors recommend that the Commission reassess whether Chips Act ambitions remain realistic given available resources, global competition, Europe's energy cost disadvantage, and raw material dependencies.

## The Flagship Projects: One Alive, Two Down

Three projects were expected to define the Chips Act's manufacturing impact. Only one is proceeding.

**TSMC Dresden — Proceeding.** The European Semiconductor Manufacturing Company (ESMC) is a joint venture between TSMC (70%), Bosch, Infineon, and NXP (30%, split equally) [5]. The €10+ billion facility received €5 billion in state aid — the largest Chips Act package approved [5]. Production on TSMC's 28/22nm CMOS and 16/12nm FinFET processes is expected to begin in 2027, with capacity of 40,000 300mm wafers per month and approximately 2,000 direct jobs.

TSMC Dresden is a genuine addition to European semiconductor capacity. But it targets mature nodes, not leading edge. The 28nm and 16nm processes it will produce are two to three generations behind the frontier — adequate for automotive, industrial, and IoT applications, but not for the AI accelerators and advanced processors that drive the highest-value segments of the market.

**Intel Magdeburg — Cancelled.** Intel's planned €30+ billion investment in two fabs in Magdeburg, Germany — backed by €10 billion in German government subsidies — was cancelled in July 2025 due to insufficient customer commitments and financial risk [6]. The cancellation was a significant blow to Europe's ambitions for leading-edge manufacturing. Intel's fabs would have been the only sub-10nm production capacity planned for EU soil.

**STMicroelectronics/GlobalFoundries Crolles — Shelved.** The €7.5 billion 300mm fab in Crolles, France, supported by €2.9 billion in French state aid, has reportedly been shelved with no progress in 18 months [7]. The companies are reportedly redirecting focus toward the Chinese market.

The scorecard is not encouraging. Of the three signature manufacturing projects, only the one targeting mature nodes is proceeding. Both leading-edge projects — which were essential to the 20% market share ambition — have been cancelled or indefinitely delayed.

## The Semiconductor Coalition and Chips Act 2.0

In March 2025, nine member states — Austria, Belgium, Finland, France, Germany, Italy, Poland, Spain, and the Netherlands — formed the Semicon Coalition [8]. By September 2025, the coalition expanded to include all 27 EU member states, signing a declaration establishing the ambition for "Chips Act 2.0" [8].

The Chips Act 2.0 proposal, expected from the European Commission in Q1 2026, aims to pool EU, national, and private funding to mobilize €200 billion in semiconductor investment by 2035 [8]. The proposal also targets training and upskilling 200,000 semiconductor professionals and attracting 10,000 foreign experts and researchers by 2030.

Chips Act 2.0 represents a tacit acknowledgment that the original act's ambitions exceeded its means. The scaling from €69 billion to €200 billion suggests the Commission has internalized the Court of Auditors' critique — that the 20% target requires investment on a scale the original legislation could not deliver.

## The Comparative Landscape

The EU Chips Act operates in a global competition for semiconductor investment where every major economy is deploying industrial policy.

**United States:** The US CHIPS Act provides $52.7 billion in funding plus $24 billion in tax incentives — approximately $76.6 billion total [9]. Critically, this is new federal money, not reallocated from existing programs. The US has also been more aggressive in linking subsidies to operational restrictions on China investment, creating clear strategic conditionality that the EU Chips Act lacks.

**Japan:** Japan's semiconductor revitalization program has committed approximately $11.6 billion to date, anchored by the Rapidus project targeting mass production of 2nm chips by 2027 [9]. Japan's broader seven-year plan proposes JPY 10 trillion (approximately $65 billion), with JPY 6 trillion in direct subsidies. Japan's approach is tightly focused on leading-edge capability, in contrast to the EU's broader investment across mature and advanced nodes.

**South Korea:** The K-Chips program provides KRW 33 trillion (approximately $23 billion) in funding, including 25% tax credits for facility investment and 50% for R&D [9]. South Korea's advantage is incumbency: Samsung and SK Hynix are already global leaders in memory and foundry, and government investment reinforces existing capability rather than building from scratch.

The EU's competitive disadvantage is structural. Energy costs in Europe are 2–3x higher than in the US and Asia. The semiconductor talent pipeline is thinner. The fragmented governance structure — 27 member states with independent industrial policies — makes coordinated execution more difficult than in unitary states. And the cancellation of Intel Magdeburg has removed the only near-term path to leading-edge manufacturing on EU soil.

## The Strategic Gap

The Chips Act's most significant gap is not funding — it is focus. The €69 billion mobilized has been spread across research, pilot lines, and manufacturing, but manufacturing investment has disproportionately favored mature nodes.

This is not irrational. Mature-node chips (28nm and above) account for a large share of European demand, particularly in automotive and industrial applications. The EU's chip shortage during 2021–2023 was primarily a mature-node shortage, and TSMC Dresden directly addresses that vulnerability.

But the strategic case for the Chips Act was about more than supply security for existing demand. It was about building capability in the advanced nodes where future value creation will concentrate — AI accelerators, high-performance computing, advanced mobile processors. Without leading-edge fab capacity, Europe remains a technology consumer rather than a technology producer at the frontier.

The pilot lines, particularly NanoIC, represent genuine research capability. IMEC is one of the world's premier semiconductor R&D institutions, and NanoIC's EUV lithography capability will contribute to the global development of sub-2nm manufacturing processes. But pilot-line research is not production. The gap between "we can develop the technology" and "we can manufacture at scale" is precisely the gap that Intel Magdeburg and the Crolles fab were supposed to bridge.

## What Chips Act 2.0 Must Address

If the original Chips Act was an emergency response to the 2021 chip shortage and the geopolitical shock of US-China decoupling, Chips Act 2.0 must be a long-term industrial strategy.

The €200 billion target suggests scale. The workforce development goals suggest awareness of the talent constraint. But the effectiveness of Chips Act 2.0 will depend on whether it addresses the structural issues the Court of Auditors identified: fragmented governance, unrealistic targets, insufficient demand analysis, and the gap between research capability and manufacturing scale.

Europe's semiconductor future likely involves accepting a more modest but realistic ambition: sustained investment in the areas where European companies already have competitive advantage — automotive chips, industrial semiconductors, advanced packaging, and the EUV equipment monopoly that ASML provides to the entire global industry — while building selective capability at the leading edge through focused partnerships rather than attempting to replicate the full-stack manufacturing ecosystems of Taiwan, South Korea, and the United States.

The 20% target was aspirational. 11.7% may be the reality. The question for Chips Act 2.0 is whether Europe can make that 11.7% strategically significant.
