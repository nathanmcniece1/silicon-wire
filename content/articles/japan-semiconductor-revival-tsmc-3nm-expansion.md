# Japan's Semiconductor Revival: TSMC Expands AI Chip Production to Third Advanced Base

**Beat:** Materials & Fab
**Format:** Wire Dispatch
**Published:** March 9, 2026
**Read time:** 5 min

**Excerpt:** TSMC has upgraded its second Kumamoto fab to produce 3nm AI chips — elevating Japan to its third advanced manufacturing base alongside Taiwan and Arizona. Combined with Rapidus's $1.7 billion 2nm push, Japan's semiconductor revival is entering a new phase.

---

Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company announced on February 5, 2026, that it will produce 3-nanometer AI semiconductors at its second plant in Kumamoto, Japan — a significant upgrade from the less advanced chips originally planned for the facility [1].

The decision elevates Japan to TSMC's third advanced chip manufacturing base, alongside Taiwan and its Arizona complex. With a ¥2.6 trillion ($17 billion) investment in the second Kumamoto fab, TSMC is signaling that Japan is no longer a peripheral node in its global manufacturing network but a strategic production center for cutting-edge AI silicon [2].

## The Kumamoto Evolution

TSMC's Japan presence has expanded rapidly. The Phase 1 JASM (Japan Advanced Semiconductor Manufacturing) fab commenced mass production in late 2024, manufacturing chips on 22/28nm and 12/16nm processes at a target capacity of 55,000 wafers per month [3]. The facility employs roughly 2,400 workers, including 527 locally hired employees as of April 2025, and runs entirely on renewable energy.

Phase 2 was originally designed for 6nm production — a mature but capable node suitable for automotive and mobile applications. The upgrade to 3nm process technology reflects the overwhelming demand for advanced AI silicon and the economic logic of deploying cutting-edge capabilities across multiple geographies [2].

The combined Phase 1 and Phase 2 investment exceeds $20 billion, with substantial Japanese government support. TSMC's 2026 capital expenditure budget of $52 to $56 billion allocates 70 to 80% to advanced process technologies, and the Kumamoto upgrade falls squarely within that priority [4].

## Rapidus: The 2nm Bet

Japan's semiconductor ambitions extend beyond attracting foreign foundries. Rapidus, the country's homegrown advanced chipmaker, secured $1.7 billion in February 2026 to advance its 2nm fabrication facility in Chitose, Hokkaido [5].

Developed in partnership with IBM, Rapidus achieved a critical milestone in July 2025 when it successfully operated its first 2nm gate-all-around transistors. The facility installed an ASML High-NA EUV lithography system in December 2024 and activated a 2nm pilot line in April 2025.

Mass production is targeted for the second half of 2027, with a second Hokkaido fab planned as early as fiscal year 2027 to produce 1.4nm chips by 2029 [5]. More than 150 Rapidus engineers trained at IBM's Albany facility in 2023–2024 to build the expertise needed for leading-edge manufacturing.

## The Government Commitment

Prime Minister Shigeru Ishiba's ¥10 trillion ($64 to $65 billion) pledge for semiconductor and AI development through 2030 provides the financial foundation. The FY 2026 budget allocates ¥1.23 trillion ($7.9 billion) for cutting-edge semiconductors and AI — a fourfold increase from previous levels [6].

Rapidus alone receives ¥630 billion as part of a broader ¥1 trillion funding package. Japan's total semiconductor subsidies since 2022 reach $25.7 billion — the largest outlay globally as a share of GDP [6].

The strategic calculus is clear: Japan views semiconductor manufacturing sovereignty as a matter of economic security. With TSMC now producing 3nm chips on Japanese soil and Rapidus targeting 2nm, Japan is building the manufacturing depth to serve its domestic AI and defense needs regardless of geopolitical disruptions to the Taiwan-centric supply chain.
