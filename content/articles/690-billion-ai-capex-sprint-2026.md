# The $690 Billion AI Capex Sprint: Where the Hyperscaler Money Is Going

**Beat:** Infrastructure
**Format:** Earnings Breakdown
**Published:** March 9, 2026
**Read time:** 13 min

**Excerpt:** Amazon, Alphabet, Microsoft, Meta, and Oracle will collectively spend $660–690 billion on capital expenditure in 2026 — nearly doubling 2025 levels. We break down who is spending what, where it's going, and why the power grid has become the binding constraint on AI's growth.

---

In roughly eighteen months, the aggregate annual AI infrastructure commitment from the five largest US cloud and technology companies has increased from approximately $380 billion in 2025 to a projected $660–690 billion in 2026 [1]. This is not an incremental increase. It is a near-doubling of capital expenditure that dwarfs anything the technology industry — or any industry — has attempted in a comparable period.

Futurum Group's analysis projects total hyperscaler AI capex at $690 billion for 2026 [1], with approximately 75% directed specifically at AI infrastructure [2]. The remaining quarter covers traditional cloud services, networking, and other business lines. IEEE ComSoc estimates the broader figure at more than $600 billion, representing a 36% increase over 2025 [3].

The numbers are so large that they have begun to distort the financial profiles of the companies spending them. Key hyperscalers are expected to spend approximately 90% of their operating cash flow on capex in 2026 — up from a historical 10-year average of around 40% [2]. Amazon's free cash flow is projected to turn negative for the year [2]. These companies are, in effect, converting their cash-generating businesses into infrastructure financing vehicles.

This is the company-by-company breakdown of where the money is going — and what's preventing it from being spent even faster.

## Amazon: $200 Billion — The Largest Corporate Capex in History

Amazon has committed to spending over $200 billion in capital expenditures in 2026 [4] — the largest single-year capex figure ever announced by any corporation. The number exceeds analyst expectations by roughly $50 billion and represents a 52% increase over the $131.8 billion Amazon spent in 2025 [4].

The vast majority flows to AWS. CEO Andy Jassy has framed the spending as demand-driven, pointing to AWS's cloud backlog of $244 billion — up 40% year-over-year [4]. AWS revenue grew 24% in Q4 2025 to $35.6 billion [5], and Amazon's internal projections suggest AI-related workloads will account for the majority of incremental demand through 2027.

The spending covers data center expansion across multiple geographies, continued scaling of Amazon's proprietary Trainium and Inferentia AI chips (manufactured by TSMC), and the networking infrastructure to connect it all. Amazon is also investing in robotics and low earth orbit satellites through Project Kuiper, though these represent a smaller share of the total [4].

The market's reaction was telling: Amazon shares fell more than 10% in after-hours trading following the announcement [4]. Investors are grappling with the tension between AI's apparent demand signal and the scale of capital required to capture it. Amazon is spending more on infrastructure in 2026 than most countries spend on their entire military budgets.

## Alphabet: $175–185 Billion — Doubling Down

Alphabet has guided 2026 capital expenditures at $175 billion to $185 billion [6] — more than double its 2025 capex spend. At the top end, Alphabet would be the second-largest infrastructure investor in the world after Amazon.

The investment targets three areas: AI compute capacity for Google DeepMind's frontier model training, scaling to meet Google Cloud customer demand, and strategic investments in "Other Bets" including Waymo's autonomous vehicle fleet [6].

In Q4 2025, approximately 60% of Alphabet's infrastructure investment went to servers and 40% to data centers and networking equipment [6]. Google Cloud's backlog reached $240 billion, up 55% year-over-year [6], providing the demand signal that justifies the spending.

Google's head of AI Infrastructure, Amin Vahdat, told staff in November 2025 that Google would need to double its AI serving capacity every six months to meet demand [7]. At that rate of growth, even $185 billion may prove insufficient.

Alphabet has also moved aggressively on energy infrastructure, acquiring Intersect, a developer of utility-scale renewable energy projects, to help secure power for its data center buildout [8]. CEO Sundar Pichai has acknowledged that supply constraints — particularly power — are the primary limitation on Alphabet's AI infrastructure growth, not demand or capital [7].

## Microsoft: $120 Billion+ — GPUs Collecting Dust

Microsoft's fiscal year 2026 capex is on an aggressive trajectory. In H1 of fiscal 2026, the company spent $72.4 billion on capital expenditures — with $37.5 billion in Q2 alone, 66% higher than the prior year [9]. If spending accelerates in H2 as expected, full-year capex could approach or exceed $100 billion.

What makes Microsoft's situation unique is the visibility into unmet demand. The company has disclosed an $80 billion backlog of Azure orders that cannot be fulfilled due to power constraints [9]. CEO Satya Nadella has admitted that NVIDIA GPUs are sitting idle in inventory because Microsoft lacks the electricity to install and operate them [9].

This is a remarkable admission: the binding constraint on AI growth is not chip supply, not capital, and not demand — it is electricity. Azure capacity constraints are expected to persist at least through the end of Microsoft's fiscal year in June 2026, and likely longer [10].

Microsoft's response has been to invest in its own power infrastructure. The company's next-generation Fairwater AI superfactories use closed-loop liquid cooling that eliminates water consumption [11]. Microsoft has signed power purchase agreements for nuclear energy, including the landmark deal to restart Three Mile Island Unit 1, and is exploring small modular reactors for dedicated data center power.

Microsoft's remaining performance obligations (its total backlog across all cloud services) doubled to $625 billion, driven largely by OpenAI and enterprise AI demand [12]. The gap between contracted demand and deliverable capacity represents the single largest infrastructure buildout opportunity — and constraint — in the cloud industry.

## Meta: $115–135 Billion — Building Tens of Gigawatts

Meta estimates 2026 full-year capex of $115 billion to $135 billion [13], up from $72.2 billion in 2025. The increase is driven by what Meta describes as "increased investment to support our Meta Superintelligence Labs efforts and core business" [13].

Meta CEO Mark Zuckerberg has stated the company plans to build "tens of gigawatts" of data center capacity this decade [14]. The company established a new division called Meta Compute to manage its AI data center ambitions [14].

Three signature projects define Meta's buildout:

**Hyperion (Louisiana):** A 2,250-acre site that will cost an estimated $10 billion and provide approximately 5 gigawatts of compute power, with an arrangement with a local nuclear power plant to handle the energy load [14].

**Prometheus (Ohio):** A smaller site expected to come online in 2026, powered by natural gas [14].

**Lebanon, Indiana:** A state-of-the-art 1 GW campus representing over $10 billion in investment, which Meta broke ground on in February 2026 [15].

Meta's infrastructure buildout supports two distinct AI workloads: training frontier Llama models (which requires massive GPU clusters running for months) and serving AI-powered advertising and recommendation systems (which requires low-latency inference at enormous scale). The company's custom MTIA chips — now targeting 35% of Meta's inference fleet by year-end — help reduce dependence on NVIDIA for the inference workload, but training still runs on NVIDIA hardware.

## Oracle: $50 Billion — The Stargate Gambit

Oracle's projected $50 billion in 2026 capex represents a 136% increase over 2025 [16] and marks the company's transformation from a database and enterprise software vendor into an AI infrastructure provider.

The driving force is Stargate — the joint venture with OpenAI and SoftBank that has grown into the largest AI infrastructure project ever announced. In July 2025, OpenAI and Oracle entered an agreement to develop up to 4.5 gigawatts of additional Stargate capacity, a partnership exceeding $300 billion over five years [17]. Combined with earlier commitments, Stargate now encompasses six US sites with nearly 7 gigawatts of planned capacity and over $400 billion in total investment [18].

Oracle's ability to execute this plan without damaging its credit rating is the key near-term question. The company's remaining performance obligations stand at $523 billion [16], providing revenue visibility, but converting that backlog into operational data centers requires navigating the same power and construction constraints facing every other hyperscaler.

If Oracle successfully brings the first 1.5 gigawatts of Stargate capacity online by late 2026, it will have accomplished something that seemed improbable two years ago: positioning itself as a top-five AI infrastructure provider alongside the four consumer-facing tech giants.

## The Power Wall

The aggregate picture reveals a binding constraint that no amount of capital can solve quickly: electricity.

Morgan Stanley Research forecasts US data center demand could reach 74 GW by 2028, with a projected shortfall of approximately 49 GW in available power access [19]. That 49 GW gap represents roughly the generation capacity of 50 large nuclear reactors — infrastructure that takes a decade or more to build.

The power constraint manifests differently for each company. Microsoft has $80 billion in orders it cannot fulfill [9]. Alphabet's CEO acknowledges that power, not capital, limits growth [7]. Amazon's $200 billion capex plan will hit grid interconnection queues in every major data center market. Meta is buying nuclear power. Oracle is building its own gas-fired generation.

PJM Interconnection, the grid operator covering 13 eastern US states, has flagged AI data center growth as its single largest demand planning challenge. New interconnection requests in Virginia's Data Center Alley alone exceed 10 gigawatts — more than the entire peak demand of many US states.

The result is a new form of competition: hyperscalers are racing not just for chips and talent, but for megawatts. Companies that secure reliable, affordable power will have a structural advantage in AI training and inference costs. Those that cannot will face the paradox of unlimited demand and unlimited capital, constrained by the physics of the electrical grid.

## Where the Money Actually Goes

Across all five hyperscalers, the $660–690 billion in 2026 capex breaks down roughly as follows:

**Servers and compute (~40%):** GPU and custom AI chip procurement from NVIDIA, AMD, and in-house silicon programs. This is the single largest category and flows directly to TSMC, HBM manufacturers, and the broader semiconductor supply chain.

**Data center construction (~25%):** Land acquisition, building construction, electrical infrastructure, and cooling systems. This drives demand for electrical equipment (Eaton, Schneider Electric), liquid cooling (Vertiv, CoolIT), and construction firms.

**Networking (~15%):** Optical transceivers, switches, and interconnect fabrics. NVIDIA's networking division (InfiniBand, Spectrum), Broadcom, Arista Networks, and Ciena are primary beneficiaries.

**Power and energy (~10%):** Power purchase agreements, on-site generation, grid interconnection, and battery storage. This is the fastest-growing category and is driving investment in nuclear, natural gas, and renewable energy infrastructure.

**Other (~10%):** Land, fiber, subsea cables, edge infrastructure, and satellite systems.

## The Sustainability Question

At $690 billion per year and growing, the central question is whether this spending trajectory is sustainable. The bull case: AI is generating measurable revenue returns (Google Cloud, AWS, and Azure are all accelerating), enterprise adoption is in early innings, and the shift from training-dominated to inference-dominated workloads will only increase infrastructure demand.

The bear case: free cash flow is evaporating, return on invested capital is declining, and the history of technology infrastructure investment is littered with examples of overbuilding — from fiber optics in 2000 to cloud computing in 2015.

The answer likely lies somewhere between. Not every dollar of the $690 billion will generate immediate returns. But the companies making these bets have something that previous infrastructure bubbles lacked: paying customers with growing demand and the cash flow to sustain multi-year commitments, even at these unprecedented scales.

What is certain is that the downstream effects are already propagating through the supply chain. TSMC's $56 billion capex budget, NVIDIA's accelerated Rubin production, SK Hynix's HBM4 expansion — all of these are responses to the same $690 billion demand signal. The hyperscaler spending wave is not just building data centers. It is reshaping the entire industrial base that produces the components those data centers require.
