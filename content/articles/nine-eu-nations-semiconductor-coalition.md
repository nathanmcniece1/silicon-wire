# Nine EU Nations Form Semiconductor Coalition as Chips Act 2.0 Looms

**Beat:** Policy & Capital
**Format:** Wire Dispatch
**Published:** March 9, 2026
**Read time:** 5 min

**Excerpt:** Austria, Belgium, Finland, France, Germany, Italy, Poland, Spain, and the Netherlands have formed a Semiconductor Coalition pushing for the EU Chips Act 2.0 — targeting €200 billion in investment by 2035. The coalition wants approval times under 7 months and a Europe-wide talent pipeline. With the original Chips Act falling short of its 20% market share target, the nine nations are pressing for a fundamental reset.

---

On March 12, 2025, nine European countries launched the European Semiconductor Coalition — a political alliance designed to accelerate the continent's chip ambitions ahead of the Chips Act 2.0 proposal expected in the first quarter of 2026 [1].

The founding members — Austria, Belgium, Finland, France, Germany, Italy, Poland, Spain, and the Netherlands — represent the core of Europe's existing semiconductor ecosystem. The Netherlands hosts ASML and NXP. Germany is home to Infineon, Bosch, and the planned TSMC-ESMC fab in Dresden. Belgium houses IMEC, Europe's premier semiconductor research institute. France anchors STMicroelectronics and Soitec.

## The Coalition's Agenda

The coalition's joint declaration outlines five priorities [2]:

**Strengthen the semiconductor ecosystem** through deeper cooperation between industry, research institutions, SMEs, and startups across member states.

**Align EU and national funding** while accelerating approval processes. The coalition targets a maximum approval timeline of under seven months for strategic European semiconductor projects — a significant compression from the multi-year bureaucratic processes that have slowed existing Chips Act investments [1].

**Build a European talent pipeline** for semiconductor technologies, addressing the workforce shortage that has constrained fab buildout across the continent.

**Emphasize energy-efficient and circular semiconductor production**, reflecting Europe's regulatory emphasis on sustainability even in strategic industrial policy.

**Enhance international cooperation with like-minded partners** while preserving European strategic autonomy — diplomatic language for balancing dependence on American, Taiwanese, and Korean technology with European industrial sovereignty.

## Why Chips Act 2.0 Is Needed

The original European Chips Act, adopted in 2023 with a target of €43 billion in public and private investment, aimed to double Europe's share of global chip production from approximately 10% to 20% by 2030. By virtually all measures, that target will not be met.

The European Court of Auditors' Special Report 12/2025 found that the EU had catalyzed €69 billion as of October 2025 — exceeding the original funding target — but that actual production capacity growth lagged far behind investment flows [3]. The Court projected Europe would reach approximately 11.7% global market share by 2030, well short of the 20% goal.

High-profile cancellations compounded the credibility problem. Intel's €30 billion Magdeburg fab was shelved. The STMicroelectronics/GlobalFoundries joint fab in France was abandoned. These were the largest planned FOAK (first-of-a-kind) facilities under the Chips Act, and their cancellation removed a significant share of projected capacity growth.

The coalition's push for Chips Act 2.0 — with a target of €200 billion by 2035 — reflects an acknowledgment that the original act was insufficient in both scale and execution speed [4].

## What Each Nation Brings

The coalition's membership is strategically composed. The Netherlands contributes ASML's lithography monopoly and NXP's automotive chip leadership. Germany offers Infineon's power semiconductor dominance and the TSMC-ESMC fab currently under construction in Dresden with European Commission state aid approval. Belgium provides IMEC's R&D infrastructure, including the €700 million NanoIC pilot line — Europe's largest Chips Act pilot facility. France contributes STMicroelectronics and Soitec's silicon-on-insulator technology.

Finland, Poland, Spain, Austria, and Italy round out the coalition with emerging semiconductor capabilities, academic research programs, and political weight within the European Council.

In September 2025, all 27 EU member states signed a broader declaration supporting the Chips Act 2.0 ambitions, but the nine-nation coalition is leading the policy design and pushing for the most aggressive timelines [2].

The question is whether political ambition can overcome the structural challenges that undermined the first Chips Act: high energy costs, complex permitting, workforce shortages, and the reality that chipmakers will build fabs where the economics favor them — which, for now, continues to be Asia and the United States.
