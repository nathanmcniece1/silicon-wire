# The 25% AI Chip Tariff: How Trump's Section 232 Proclamation Reshapes Semiconductor Trade

**Beat:** Policy & Capital
**Format:** Wire Dispatch
**Published:** March 9, 2026
**Read time:** 5 min

**Excerpt:** President Trump imposed a 25% value-based tariff on advanced AI chips not destined for the US supply chain, effective January 15, 2026. The Section 232 proclamation targets NVIDIA H200-class and AMD MI325X-class accelerators but exempts chips imported for US data center buildout, R&D, and domestic manufacturing. Here's how the tariff works.

---

On January 14, 2026, President Trump signed a Presidential Proclamation under Section 232 imposing a 25% value-based tariff on "covered products" — defined as advanced AI computing chips, including NVIDIA H200-class and AMD MI325X-class accelerators — not destined for the US supply chain [1].

The tariff took effect January 15, 2026.

## What's Covered

The proclamation targets a narrow but economically significant category: the most advanced AI accelerator chips that are manufactured abroad (primarily at TSMC in Taiwan) and would otherwise be shipped to non-US data centers, cloud providers, or end users.

The 25% tariff applies to the declared value of covered chips at the point of import. For chips in the $30,000 to $40,000 per-unit range (H200-class), this adds $7,500 to $10,000 per chip — a material cost increase for international buyers [2].

## What's Exempt

The exemption list is deliberately broad, reflecting the administration's intent to penalize international chip flows while protecting domestic AI infrastructure investment [1]:

- Chips imported for US data center buildout
- Chips imported for US repairs or replacements
- Chips destined for US research and development
- Chips purchased by US startups
- Non-data-center consumer applications in the US
- Non-data-center civil industrial applications in the US
- US public sector applications
- Any other use the Secretary of Commerce deems beneficial to US technology supply chains or domestic manufacturing capacity

The exemption framework effectively creates a two-tier pricing system for advanced AI chips: US-destined chips enter tariff-free, while chips destined for non-US markets face a 25% surcharge.

## The Strategic Logic

The tariff serves multiple policy objectives simultaneously.

**Revenue generation:** At current volumes, the tariff could generate billions in annual revenue from the global AI chip trade, though the exact figure depends on enforcement and behavioral response.

**Demand onshoring:** By making it 25% more expensive to deploy advanced AI chips outside the US, the tariff creates a financial incentive for hyperscalers, cloud providers, and AI companies to build data center capacity on American soil rather than abroad. This aligns with the administration's broader goal of concentrating AI infrastructure within US borders.

**Export control complement:** The tariff operates alongside — not instead of — existing export controls. Chips that are prohibited from export to certain countries (such as China) remain prohibited. The tariff adds a cost layer to chips flowing to allied nations that are not subject to export restrictions but are not the US.

## Industry Response

The semiconductor industry's initial reaction has been measured. NVIDIA, AMD, and other chipmakers have noted that the tariff's broad exemptions mean the majority of their US-bound revenue is unaffected. The impact falls primarily on international customers who source chips through US distribution channels.

For TSMC, which manufactures the majority of covered chips, the tariff has no direct effect — the duty is assessed on import, not manufacturing. But it may accelerate TSMC's Arizona fab buildout by increasing the strategic value of chips manufactured on US soil, which would presumably be exempt from the tariff.

The longer-term question is whether the tariff shifts global AI infrastructure investment patterns. If deploying AI chips in the US is structurally cheaper than deploying them in Europe, the Middle East, or Southeast Asia, the tariff could concentrate an even larger share of global AI compute capacity within the United States — reinforcing the geographic advantage that US hyperscalers already enjoy.
